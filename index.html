Cognitive Debt
An Interactive Story
Maya is a junior. It's past midnight. There's a paper due in a few hours and she just found a tool that could fix her problem — right now, tonight.

What she does next seems completely reasonable to her. Most people in her position would do the same thing.
Act I  ·  2:17 AM

Maya is an English major—the kind who actually does the readings and writes all over the pages with notes. She cares a lot and tries hard to produce her best work in everything she does.

But right now she’s been stuck on a blank document for forty minutes. She knows what she wants to say—postcolonial theory, Beloved, all of that. It’s in her head. She just can’t get it to come out in a way that works or sounds right to her.

Someone in class said something last week about some tool that was helpful for them. “You just drop your idea in and it builds an outline. You still write everything—it just helps you start.”

She stares at the cursor for a second… then opens a new tab.

AI response — 3.8 seconds

Here is a suggested outline for your argument that postcolonial theory in Beloved reframes the Western literary archive.

Begin with the body as a site of memory — how Morrison represents history through physical experience.

Then move into the uncanny as a form of historical return, where the past disrupts the present in unsettling ways.

Finally, consider the limits of written testimony, and what remains unresolved when experience cannot be fully captured in language...

She fills in the sections herself, finds her own sentences, makes it sound like her. She submits at 4:09 AM.

She gets a B+.

She tells herself it was just this once.

What the research shows — first use
MIT measured students' actual brains. The AI group showed up to 55% reduced neural connectivity compared to those writing on their own.
MIT Media Lab researchers ran 54 people ages 18–39 through four writing sessions, split into three groups: write independently, use a search engine, or use ChatGPT. Real-time EEG measured brain activity the entire time. The AI group showed dramatically lower neural connectivity, weaker memory recall of their own work, and the lowest reported sense of ownership over what they'd written. The researchers named it "cognitive debt" — convenience now, reduced capacity later. One finding stood out: the order matters. Students who wrote independently first, then switched to AI later, showed far stronger brain activity than those who started with AI from the beginning.
Kosmyna et al. (2025), MIT Media Lab — arXiv:2506.08872, NIH/PMC indexed MIT Media Lab project page NIH/PMC summary
Act II  ·  The Habit

It doesn’t feel like a habit is forming.

It feels like making a reasonable choice, over and over again.


Discussion board post due by midnight?

She’s tired. It’s the same kind of prompt every week. She pastes her notes in, cleans up what comes back, done.

Email to a professor that needs to land exactly right?

She writes a draft, then runs it through the tool to smooth it out.

Getting started on a paper?

She knows what she’s arguing. She just needs the skeleton first.


What she doesn’t notice from the inside is that she used to have a whole process for this:

Stare at the ceiling.

Get annoyed.

Walk around.

Sit back down.

Even though it felt like wasted time, it was actually where most of her thinking happened.


Now she opens the tab before even trying on her own.

What the research shows — the habit
666 people studied. Heavy AI use showed a statistically significant negative correlation with critical thinking scores — and the 17–25 age group was hit hardest.
A 2025 peer-reviewed study in Societies (MDPI open access) measured AI usage habits against standardized critical thinking assessments across 666 participants. The finding: frequent AI use correlated significantly with lower critical thinking performance. The mechanism is cognitive offloading — the brain stops practicing the reasoning it's handing off. The effect was most pronounced in the 17–25 age group, and it was driven specifically by how often users engaged with AI output passively, without critically processing it themselves.
Gerlich, M. (2025). Societies, 15(1), 6. MDPI open access. Read the full study (open access)
Act III  ·  The Memory Slip

Her seminar professor has a habit of calling on someone and putting them on the spot randomly — no warning, just turns to someone mid-discussion and asks them to pick up the thread. Most students find it stressful, but Maya used to find it kind of exciting. She’d retained the material, had opinions about it, and could usually make something happen on the spot.

Today her professor turns to her. They're discussing the very paper she got a B+ on — the one she built on an AI outline, filled in herself and thought sounded solid.

She could describe the argument. But when her professor pushes — “okay, but how does that connect to what you said last week?” — Maya draws a blank. Not the normal kind of blank where you just need a second. A real blank. Like the knowledge is there somewhere but the thread back to it is gone.

After class she opens her submitted paper and reads it. It's good. It's organized. She barely remembers writing any of it.

"Did I actually learn any of this?"
What the research shows — memory
Prolonged AI exposure led to measurable memory decline in undergraduates — even when students performed well on the AI-assisted work.
A Penn State study of 73 information science undergraduates divided participants into two groups: one used AI directly; the other did a pretest first, then used AI. The pretest group showed significantly better retention and recall on follow-up assessments. But prolonged AI exposure in the direct-use group led to measurable memory decline — not just on the material, but on their own outputs. A PMC/NIH-indexed cognitive review (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025) confirmed the mechanism: AI reduces the active recall and problem-solving engagement that are essential for long-term memory formation. The grade can look fine. The learning may not have happened at all.
Akgun & Toker (2024), arXiv:2412.13487; Jose et al. (2025), Frontiers in Psychology — PMC indexed. Penn State pretesting study Cognitive paradox review (PMC)
Act IV  ·  Static

It starts small. Maya's in a study group and someone asks what she thinks about the reading — just casually, in the middle of a conversation. She knows she has thoughts about it, but she can’t access them quickly enough, not the way she used to be able to, and the conversation moves on before she finds the words.

She notices it happening in other situations too. Speaking up in class feels harder than it used to. Not because she's more anxious — she was never really anxious — but because the gap between having an idea and being able to say it clearly, on the spot, without drafting it first, has gotten wider.

She catches herself wanting to type something out and run it through the tool before she says it out loud. Which obviously she can't do in real time.

"I used to just talk. When did talking start feeling like a draft I hadn't revised yet?"
What the research shows — social and interpersonal effects
Researchers documented "hampered social adaptability" and "hampered interpersonal interactions" as direct outcomes of AI over-reliance in students.
A 2025 peer-reviewed systematic review in Research and Practice in Technology Enhanced Learning analyzed psychological impacts of AI use across student populations. Among the documented negative outcomes: hampered social adaptability, induced anxiety, and hampered interpersonal interactions — particularly in unscripted, real-time conversation. A separate Frontiers in Psychology review (2025, PMC-indexed) found that over-reliance on AI "may compromise essential interpersonal skills and emotional intelligence," especially when AI regularly mediates how students articulate ideas before communicating them. The concern isn't that these students become antisocial — it's that spontaneous expression, the kind that happens in seminars and conversations and job interviews, becomes harder to access.
Kundu & Bej (2025), Research and Practice in Technology Enhanced Learning; Klimova & Pikhart (2025), Frontiers in Psychology — PMC. Psychological impacts review (RPTEL) Student well-being review (PMC)
Act V  ·  The Wall

The take-home exam is open-notes, open-book — but no internet. On her honor. Maya sits down, reads the prompt, and freezes.

She understands the question. She's done the reading. She even has things she could say. But without the tool, nothing arranges itself. No opening sentence appears. No clean structure rises up.

The blank page doesn’t just feel uncomfortable anymore — it feels like a wall.

She writes one sentence, hates it, deletes it. Writes another, deletes that too. After twenty minutes, she realizes the problem isn’t that she has no ideas.

The problem is that she’s gotten used to something else turning that first messy part into something usable.

It feels like mental debt. Every time the tool made writing easier, it also let her skip a small piece of the struggle. Now all of that skipped struggle is due at once.

Then in workshop, a student named Reese reads an essay out loud. It's messy — the structure wanders, and there’s a clunky sentence near the middle. But there’s a paragraph near the end about her grandmother that makes the room go quiet.

Maya feels something she hasn’t felt about writing in months. Not jealousy of the grade. Jealousy of the ownership. Reese’s essay is imperfect, but it belongs to her.

Maya goes home. Opens her current draft. Reads it.

It is clean. It is organized. It would probably get a decent grade.

But she can barely remember making any of the choices inside it.

Now she has to decide what to do about it.

What the research shows — self-efficacy and voice
58% of students admitted dishonest AI use. What professors flag most often isn't grammar — it's the absence of voice and argumentative risk.
The International Center for Academic Integrity (2024) found 58% of students had used AI to complete work in ways their institution would consider dishonest. A peer-reviewed study in Frontiers in Education (2025) found that instructors most commonly identify AI-assisted work not through grammar errors but through the flattening of individual voice and the disappearance of risky, unconventional thinking. Separately, a 2025 NIH/PMC study found that AI dependency reduces academic performance primarily through lowered self-efficacy — students stop believing they can do the work themselves, and that lost confidence becomes self-fulfilling on unassisted, high-stakes assessments.

Ending One  ·  Reclamation
What happens
It comes back slower than she expects.

Maya deletes the draft. Closes the tab. Sits with the blank page again.

This time, nothing comes easier. The first paragraph takes almost an hour. It feels worse than anything she’s written all semester — less polished, less certain. She keeps wanting to open the tab. She doesn’t.

The second paragraph is a little faster. Not good. Just… possible.

Somewhere in the middle, she writes a sentence that surprises her. Not because it’s perfect, but because it feels like something she actually thought through herself.

She finishes the paper knowing it’s uneven. There are gaps. Transitions that don’t quite land. But she can trace where each idea came from. She remembers writing it.

The grade is fine. Not amazing.

What stays with her is that the wall is still there — but now she knows she can push through it. Slowly, the pieces she skipped start coming back.

This ending treats writing ability as something that can atrophy when it isn’t used — and something that has to be rebuilt with friction. The difficulty doesn’t go away immediately. That’s the point.
Ending Two  ·  Ambiguity
What happens
The rules work — until they don’t.

Maya sets rules. No AI for first drafts. No AI when she hasn’t already tried to start. Use it only after something exists on the page.

At first, it helps. The first few assignments feel closer to how writing used to feel — slower, but more hers. The tool becomes something she checks against, not something she begins with.

But the line moves in small ways. A long week. A late night. She tells herself she just needs help getting started — just this once.

The next time, it’s easier to make the same exception.

She still writes more than she did before. She still catches herself sometimes and pulls back. But she also notices how quickly the shortcut becomes the default again when she’s tired.

By the end of the semester, she has some of her voice back.

She also knows how easily she could lose it again.

This ending doesn’t resolve cleanly because the behavior doesn’t either. Managing a tool that removes friction is easy to define in theory and difficult to maintain in practice — especially under real deadlines.
Ending Three  ·  Dependency
What happens
It works. That’s what makes it hard to notice.

Maya decides to stop fighting it. Writing, to her, is now about directing something that produces clean structure quickly. She gets better at prompting, better at editing, better at making the output sound like her.

Her grades stay high. Her papers are clear, organized, consistent.

The wall never really comes back — because she doesn’t let herself hit it.

Later, in a class that doesn’t allow any outside tools, she sits down to write again.

The same blank page. The same feeling.

She realizes she hasn’t practiced starting from nothing in a long time. The first sentence feels harder than it used to. Not impossible. Just unfamiliar.

She finishes something. It works.

But it takes longer than she expects, and it never quite feels like it belongs to her in the same way it used to.

This ending isn’t failure in the obvious sense. It raises a quieter question: what happens when a skill still appears intact, but the ability to perform it independently has been gradually reduced?
